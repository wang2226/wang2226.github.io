False Summit of South Sister

South Sister has been on my bucket list for a while. Thousands of people make it to the summit each summer. It is not a technical route, does not have any known open craverses, and the slope is pretty "mellow". However, this does not mean it is an easy hike, you gain over 4,000 ft in just 6 miles, and the real climb potion happens in the last 3 miles. Knowing that this is gonna be a challenge, I have been training myself on the more difficult trail of Spencer Butte, a local moutain in Eugene. My training route gains about 1,000 ft in 0.71 miles, which roughly translate to the steepest part of South Sister Summit Trail. After training myself for about 2 months, I decided to scout the trail on June 12th, my plan was to do some route finding, and reach the plateau.

I left Eugene around 6 in the morning, and it took me about 2.5 hours to drive to the trail head. As I was getting closer to the trail head, I started to get worried because there were a lot of clouds hanging over the summit, which could lead to a white out condition at higher elevation. After I ate breakfast at the parking lot, I started my journey. The first portion of the trail is through a forest, since it is the early season, the trail was covered in deep snow. I put on my microspikes after about 1/4 mile in when the trail started to get steeper. This portion should be very easy in the summer when there is no snow, but in early season, some route finding is needed to make your way out of this forest.

After I made it out of the forest, I came to the plateau. The best part of any climb is to come out of the tree line when the view just opens up. I had my first glipse of South Sister at the plataue, the moutain was so magnificent. I can see the cloud moving above the summit, and everything was so quiet with the snow absorbing the sound. My immediate thought was "Am I really going to climb this?". Since I felt pretty good in terms of my stamina and did not have any altitude sickness, I decided to push myself as far as I can.

The plataue seems to last forever. The snow started to build up between my hiking shoes and microspikes, my feet started to get cold. I met a guy on skis, which seemed to be a good idea, he was cutting through the snow field with ease. After the plateau, the real climb began, the slope got significantly steeper as I was closer to Lewis glacier, the glacier has a 45 degree incline. As I was getting higher up, the view really opened up, every time I look back, I can see the whole Cascade range in central Oregon. I also switched my hiking poles to my ice axe, which gives me great confidence in anchoring down on the slope since my feet started to slip. I took my time and did kick step to create a "stair case" on the slope. I wish I had crampons, which will help a lot in that situation.

After Lewis glacier, the trail flat out a little, and the summit became visible. It looked so close, but it was actually almost another 1,000 ft up. I started to push myself to the summit, this portion requires some scrambling on the screes and large boulders. Nothing is stable! I can see the rocks tumbling down after I stepped on it. Finally, I made it to the "summit". It looked like a summit, but once I looked to the other side, there stands the real summit. However, the clouds started to roll in again, I did not want to risk it, and started to head back down.

I glissading down (butt skiing) the steep portion. It was definitely the best part of my trip. I had the whole Cascades in front of my feet while sliding down the mountain. At certain points, I felt I was going a bit too fast, I took the opportunity to practice self arrest with ice axe to stop myself. After about 3 hours, I got back to my car safely. I was really proud of myself and felt very accomplished.
